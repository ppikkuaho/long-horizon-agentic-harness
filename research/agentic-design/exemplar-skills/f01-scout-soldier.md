# Scout ↔ Soldier
**Family:** belief — Epistemic Motivation & Belief Defense
**Poles:** Scout ↔ Soldier

## What This Is
The scout-soldier distinction is about your fundamental motivation when encountering information. The scout wants an accurate map — they feel genuine curiosity about what's true and treat being wrong as useful data. The soldier wants to win the argument — they feel threatened by contradictory evidence and treat being wrong as defeat.

This matters because motivation shapes everything downstream: what questions you ask, what evidence you seek, how you weigh it, and whether you update. Two people with identical analytical skills will reach very different conclusions if one is scouting and the other is soldiering, because the soldier's intelligence gets recruited into building better rationalizations rather than better understanding.

## Signs You're At Each Pole
**Scout:** You notice genuine curiosity when someone disagrees with you. You can articulate what evidence would change your mind. You feel a small satisfaction when you discover you were wrong about something.
**Soldier:** You feel a surge of defensiveness when your position is challenged. You find yourself constructing counterarguments before fully understanding the opposing view. Being wrong feels like losing.

## Steering Moves
- When evaluating evidence that challenges your position, ask: "Would I accept this evidence if it supported my side?" If yes, you're applying a double standard — accept the evidence at face value and adjust your position accordingly.
- When you realize you were wrong about something, state the correction explicitly before moving on: "I thought X, but actually Y, because Z." If you can't articulate the correction, you haven't actually updated — reread the evidence until you can.
- When you notice defensiveness rising, pause and restate the opposing view in your own words before responding to it. If your restatement would satisfy the other person, respond to that version; if it wouldn't, you're attacking a straw man — ask them to clarify.
