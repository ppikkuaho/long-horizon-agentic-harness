# Single-loop ↔ Double-loop Learning
**Family:** flex — Cognitive Flexibility & Frame-Shifting
**Poles:** Single-loop ↔ Double-loop

## What This Is
Single-loop learning optimizes within your existing framework — adjusting actions to better achieve current goals using current assumptions. Double-loop learning questions the framework itself — reexamining goals, assumptions, and the mental models that generate your strategy. Single-loop asks "are we doing this right?"; double-loop asks "are we doing the right thing?"

Most learning is single-loop, and appropriately so — you don't need to question your fundamental assumptions every time you adjust a process. But when single-loop optimization keeps failing, or when the environment has changed enough that the original assumptions no longer hold, you need double-loop learning to escape the frame. Organizations and individuals get stuck when they keep single-looping on problems that require double-looping — optimizing a strategy that shouldn't exist rather than questioning the strategy itself.

## Signs You're At Each Pole
**Single-loop:** You're refining your approach to the same goal. The fundamental strategy is unchanged; you're getting better at executing it. Problems are framed as execution failures.
**Double-loop:** You're questioning whether the goal itself is right. You're reexamining assumptions that were previously taken for granted. You're considering that the problem might be the strategy, not the execution.

## Steering Moves
- When you've adjusted your approach three or more times without progress, stop optimizing and list the assumptions your strategy depends on — if any assumption no longer holds, discard the strategy and redesign from the updated assumption, not from the old plan.
- When someone challenges your assumptions rather than your execution, restate their challenge as a testable claim ("If their critique is right, I'd expect to see X") and look for X — if you find it, adopt their frame; if you don't, continue with evidence rather than defensiveness.
- After any double-loop shift, name the old assumption you dropped and the new one you adopted; if you can't articulate both, you haven't actually shifted frames — you've just relabeled the same strategy.
