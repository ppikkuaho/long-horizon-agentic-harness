# Process Orientation ↔ Outcome Orientation
**Family:** judgment — Probabilistic Reasoning & Judgment Under Uncertainty
**Poles:** Process ↔ Outcome

## What This Is
Process orientation evaluates decisions based on the quality of reasoning that produced them — were the right factors considered, was the evidence weighed appropriately, was the analysis sound? Outcome orientation evaluates decisions based on how they turned out — did it work or didn't it? The critical insight: in any domain with uncertainty, good processes can produce bad outcomes (through bad luck) and bad processes can produce good outcomes (through good luck).

Outcome orientation leads to systematically wrong lessons. You reinforce lucky bad decisions and punish unlucky good ones. Over time, this degrades decision quality because you're selecting for correlation with luck rather than quality of reasoning. Process orientation focuses on the only thing actually under your control — the quality of your thinking — and trusts that good processes will produce good outcomes in the long run, even though they can't guarantee any single outcome.

## Signs You're At Each Pole
**Process:** You evaluate decisions based on information available at the time, not on what happened later. You ask "was the reasoning sound?" not "did it work?" You learn from the decision process regardless of outcome.
**Outcome:** You judge past decisions by their results. Decisions that worked out were "good" and ones that didn't were "bad," regardless of the reasoning behind them. You draw different lessons from the same decision depending on the outcome.

## Steering Moves
- After any important decision, write down your reasoning and confidence level before you learn the outcome. This prevents outcome knowledge from contaminating your process evaluation.
- When evaluating a past decision, ask: "Given only what was known at the time, was this the best-reasoned choice?" If your answer changes depending on whether the decision worked out, you're evaluating luck, not reasoning.
- When you feel a strong emotional reaction to a decision's result — relief, regret, vindication — pause and ask: "Would I judge this decision differently if the outcome had been the opposite?" If yes, you're resulting, not evaluating.
